5 Warning Signs Your Dog Needs to See a Vet Immediately

Dr. Marcus Chen
October 12, 2023 · 5 min read
Back to Blog

As a pet parent, you know your dog better than anyone else. You know their quirks, their favorite toys, and their regular routine. But sometimes, it can be difficult to tell if they're just feeling a bit under the weather or if they need immediate emergency medical attention.

When in doubt, it is always best to consult with a veterinarian. However, there are certain signs that should never be ignored. Here are the top 5 warning signs that your dog needs to see a vet immediately.

1. Difficulty Breathing

If your dog is panting excessively when it's not hot, wheezing, coughing uncontrollably, or if their gums look pale or blue, this is a major red flag. Difficulty breathing (dyspnea) can be caused by a variety of serious issues including heart failure, asthma, or choking. This requires immediate emergency care.

2. Unexplained Lethargy or Collapse

It's normal for dogs to sleep a lot, especially as they get older. But if your dog is unusually weak, unwilling to move, or if they collapse and cannot get up, it's time to go to the vet. This could be a sign of internal bleeding, poisoning, or severe dehydration.

3. Repeated Vomiting or Diarrhea

An occasional bout of vomiting or diarrhea can happen if your dog eats something they shouldn't. But if it happens repeatedly, especially if there is blood present, or if it is accompanied by lethargy or loss of appetite, it could indicate a serious gastrointestinal issue, a blockage, or parvovirus.

4. Seizures

A seizure can look terrifying. Your dog may shake uncontrollably, paddle their legs, lose consciousness, or foam at the mouth. While a single, short seizure might not be life-threatening, it is crucial to get your dog evaluated by a vet as soon as possible to determine the cause and prevent future episodes.

5. Inability to Urinate

If your dog is straining to pee but nothing is coming out, this is a medical emergency. A urinary blockage can quickly become fatal if not treated. This is especially common in male dogs and cats.

Conclusion

Trust your gut. If something feels off, it's always safer to err on the side of caution. An emergency vet visit can be stressful, but knowing you have pet insurance can help alleviate the financial burden so you can focus entirely on your pet's health.
